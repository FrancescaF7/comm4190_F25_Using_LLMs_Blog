LLM as a Branding Team

Can an LLM think like a branding team?
LLMs
Creativity
Marketing
Author

Francesca

Published

October 19, 2025

In my marketing class, we have spoken in depth about brand naming, brand positioning, marketing, target segements, and everything in-between. It made me think about how good an LLM would be at doing this. Could an LLM ensure no terrible brand decisions were made? Could they be a company’s CMO?

On a smaller scale - for the sake of this blogpost - I wanted to see if an LLM could do what creative agencies spend weeks doing: generate names that sound human, memorable, and just a little bit clever.

I gave ChatGPT a simple brief:

“You are a brand strategist. Generate 10 names for a luggage company that’s stylish, functional, and appeals to young travelers.”

The results came back instantly:

Glide

JetSet

Nomadica

CarryOn & Co.

The Atlas Project

Not bad, but also… not good. They sounded like ideas from a brainstorming session that ended very late. The model clearly understood form — short, sleek, aspirational — but not feeling.

Next, I added constraints:

“The brand should feel luxurious but minimal. Avoid clichés like ‘jet,’ ‘nomad,’ or ‘travel.’ Think Béis meets Apple.”

Suddenly, the tone shifted:

Paravel

Onyx

Vale

In Transit

Haul

Still imperfect, but closer. It started understanding what I meant by vibe — the emotional texture behind a brand, not just the dictionary meaning. When I added follow-up instructions like “make it sound feminine but not fragile” or “use soft phonetics,” it began producing names that actually sounded on-brand.

The real magic appeared when I turned the prompt into a conversation:

Me: “That sounds a bit too techy.” LLM: “Would you prefer something softer, maybe inspired by natural materials?” Me: “Yes — something that feels tactile, not digital.” LLM: “How about Loom, Drift, or Sonder?”

At that point, it didn’t feel like a generator. It felt like a collaborator.

What I Learned

LLMs aren’t great at originality on their own — but they’re excellent mirrors for refining creative direction. They give you quick volume, variety, and vocabulary, which you can then sculpt into something uniquely yours.

They’re especially useful for:

Brainstorming names when you’re stuck.

Testing how language feels in context.

Exploring cross-language ideas (e.g., “What would a French or Latin-inspired name sound like?”).

The secret isn’t to expect brilliance — it’s to use the LLM as a sounding board that helps you see what you like.
